"How much should I spend on a wedding gift?" is one of the most universally googled social questions. And for good reason — get it badly wrong in either direction and you risk giving offence or damaging a relationship. Too little reads as cheap or dismissive; too much creates awkward social imbalance.

The honest answer is: it depends on four factors. This guide breaks down each one and gives you practical reference ranges for every major occasion and cultural context.

The Four Factors That Affect the Right Gift Amount

1

Relationship closeness

The closer the relationship, the higher the expected gift value. A best friend's wedding warrants more than a colleague's. An immediate family member's birthday warrants more than an acquaintance's.

2

Occasion significance

A 50th wedding anniversary is a bigger occasion than a casual birthday gathering. A first home purchase is a bigger milestone than a third. Scale your gift to the weight of the occasion.

3

Cultural and social norms

Every culture has implicit gift norms. Indian weddings expect higher cash amounts than British ones. Chinese red envelopes have symbolic number expectations. Knowing the norm prevents under or over-shooting.

4

Gift history

What did they give you at your equivalent event? This is your most reliable guide. GiftKhata's person-wise ledger tells you instantly — no memory required.

Using Gift History to Make Better Decisions

The tables above give you a starting framework. But your best guide is always your own relationship history with the person. ✅

The reciprocity principle: When unsure, match or slightly exceed what the person gave you at your equivalent event. If they gave £100 at your wedding, give £100–£120 at theirs. If they gave generously for your child's birthday, match it for theirs. Your GiftKhata records make this calculation effortless.

Key Takeaway

There's no single "right" gift amount — but there are cultural norms, relationship expectations, and your own personal history to guide you.
